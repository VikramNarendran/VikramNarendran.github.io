Beyond the QRH: Why I'm Building MADSS for Aviation Safety

📅 April 2026 ⏱ 8 min read ✍️ Dr. Vikram Narendran

For 25 years, I've watched pilots rely on Quick Reference Handbooks (QRH) during emergencies. These documents are brilliant—comprehensive, regulatory-approved, and proven effective. But they have one critical limitation: they're sequential. A pilot under stress follows procedures linearly, often missing connections between related procedures or failing to recognize warnings embedded in other documents.

This is where MADSS comes in.

The Problem: Sequential Decision-Making Under Stress

In a cockpit emergency, cognitive load is extreme. The human brain operates in a narrowed attention window. A pilot executing a QRH procedure for an engine fire might miss a related advisory in the FCOM about fuel system interactions, or overlook a MEL note about minimum equipment restrictions. These aren't pilot failures—they're human limitations amplified by stress.

Generic LLMs like GPT-4 can't solve this. Why? Because they lack domain-specific knowledge. They don't understand the nuanced relationship between QRH procedures, FCOM limitations, and MEL constraints. Ask GPT-4 about a specific Boeing 777 engine fire scenario, and it might generate plausible-sounding but incorrect guidance. In aviation, "plausible" is dangerous.

The Solution: Multi-Document Agentic AI

MADSS is built on three pillars:

  • Multi-Document Retrieval (RAG): The system indexes every relevant document—QRH, FCOM, MEL, Service Bulletins. When a pilot enters an emergency, MADSS retrieves ALL related information, not just one procedure.
  • Agentic Decision-Making: MADSS doesn't just retrieve documents; it reasons about them. It identifies relationships, highlights conflicts, and flags critical warnings. It acts like a highly experienced check captain sitting beside the pilot.
  • Aviation Domain Fine-Tuning: Built on a Small Language Model (SLM) trained specifically on aviation procedures, not a generic LLM. The model understands cockpit terminology, regulatory constraints, and safety-critical decision patterns.

Validation with 300+ Aviation Professionals

We validated MADSS with 300 aviation professionals—pilots, flight engineers, training captains, and regulatory inspectors. Using PLS-SEM analysis, we measured:

  • Decision accuracy (MADSS guidance vs. traditional QRH)
  • Cognitive load reduction
  • Procedure completeness (did pilots follow all necessary steps?)
  • Confidence in decision outcomes

The results were compelling. MADSS demonstrated superior performance in identifying related procedures and flagging potential procedural conflicts—situations where traditional QRH usage could lead to incomplete or unsafe execution.

Why This Matters for Aviation Safety

Aviation is one of the safest industries precisely because we've invested in training, procedures, and continuous improvement. MADSS isn't about replacing human judgment—it's about augmenting it. It gives pilots access to institutional knowledge at the moment of highest need, when cognitive resources are most strained.

The future of aviation safety isn't about removing humans from the loop. It's about giving them better tools to think, decide, and act under extreme pressure.

How Random Forest Cut Simulator Downtime by 35%

📅 March 2026 ⏱ 12 min read ✍️ Dr. Vikram Narendran

Full Flight Simulators (FFS) are expensive—each unit costs millions. For training centers like CAE, downtime isn't just costly; it's operationally catastrophic. Every hour a simulator is offline is an hour of training slots lost, scheduled courses delayed, revenue deferred.

When I arrived at CAE as Senior FSTD Engineer, we were managing downtime reactively. A component failed, we fixed it. But we weren't predicting failures.

The Data We Had

CAE operates 20+ simulators across multiple aircraft types. Each simulator generates massive operational data:

  • Monthly maintenance logs
  • Component failure history
  • Operating hours per system
  • Environmental conditions
  • Maintenance intervention records

We had 10 years of historical data. The question: could we predict failures before they happened?

Building the Predictive Model

We chose Random Forest—an ensemble learning algorithm that excels at non-linear relationships and handling mixed data types. Here's why:

  • Handles Mixed Features: Our data included continuous variables (operating hours), categorical variables (component types), and temporal patterns. Random Forest handles this heterogeneity elegantly.
  • Feature Importance: Unlike black-box models, Random Forest tells us which features drive predictions. This helps maintenance planners prioritize interventions.
  • Robust to Outliers: Maintenance data is messy. Random Forest is resistant to anomalies and doesn't require heavy preprocessing.

The Implementation Challenge

Predicting simulator failures isn't trivial. Components fail at different rates, interact with each other, and are affected by seasonal patterns (simulators get used more during certain training cycles).

We engineered features that captured:

  • Days since last maintenance for each component
  • Failure rate trends (accelerating vs. stable)
  • Correlation between component failures
  • Utilization intensity (operating hours per day)
  • Seasonal patterns

Results: 35% Downtime Reduction

After training on 10 years of historical data, the model achieved:

  • 72% prediction accuracy for component failures 30 days in advance
  • CAD 250,000+ saved annually through reduced downtime and optimized maintenance scheduling
  • 35% reduction in unplanned downtime in the first year of deployment
  • Improved maintenance scheduling: Instead of reactive maintenance, we transitioned to predictive maintenance, reducing emergency service calls by 45%

How It Works in Practice

The system runs monthly. It analyzes each simulator's operational data and generates a risk score for each major subsystem. Maintenance teams receive a ranked list: "Motion system on A320 simulator: HIGH RISK. Recommend hydraulic filter replacement this week." This allows preventive maintenance before failures occur.

Key Learnings

1. Domain Knowledge Matters

The best features weren't the ones data scientists initially proposed. They were the ones engineering teams suggested—metrics that reflected decades of hands-on maintenance experience.

2. Model Interpretability is Critical

Maintenance teams don't trust a black box saying "replace this." They want to know why. Random Forest's feature importance output was crucial for adoption. When we said "the model recommends hydraulic replacement because this component has historically failed 6 months after this maintenance interval," teams believed it.

3. Organizational Change Matters More Than Algorithms

The hardest part wasn't building the model. It was convincing the maintenance team to schedule preventive work based on algorithmic recommendations. We solved this through workshops, showed historical examples where the model would have caught failures, and invited feedback. Adoption took 3 months.

The real value of ML isn't intelligence—it's action. A brilliant model that's ignored is worthless. A simple model that drives better decisions is invaluable.

What's Next

We're expanding the model to include environmental factors (temperature, humidity) and exploring deep learning approaches for time-series prediction. But honestly, the Random Forest approach works so well that we're being cautious about complexity. Sometimes the best model is the one your team understands and trusts.

Domain-Specific SLMs vs Generic LLMs: A Pilot's Perspective

📅 February 2026 ⏱ 10 min read ✍️ Dr. Vikram Narendran

The hype around GPT-4 and other large language models is justified—they're impressive. But ask a pilot or flight engineer if they'd trust ChatGPT to help them handle an in-flight emergency, and they'll give you a definitive "no."

Why? Because domain matters. Spectacularly.

The Case Against Generic LLMs in Safety-Critical Domains

Problem 1: Knowledge Gaps

Generic LLMs are trained on broad internet data. They know about aviation the way a tourist knows about Paris—surface-level understanding, disconnected facts. A generic LLM knows that aircraft have engines, but doesn't understand the nuanced differences between an Airbus FADEC (Full Authority Digital Engine Control) system and a Boeing engine bleed architecture.

Problem 2: Hallucinations in High-Stakes Domains

LLM hallucination—confidently generating plausible-sounding but false information—is tolerable in creative writing. It's catastrophic in emergency procedures. In our tests, GPT-4 generated procedure steps that sounded authentic but violated actual Airbus procedures. A pilot following this guidance could worsen an emergency.

Problem 3: Lack of Regulatory Awareness

Aviation operates under strict regulatory frameworks: EASA, FAA, ICAO standards. Procedures must be compliant. Generic LLMs don't understand regulatory nuance. They don't know that a specific MEL (Minimum Equipment List) entry is binding, not optional, or that certain checklists are legally required in certain jurisdictions.

Enter Small Language Models (SLMs)

A Small Language Model is a domain-specific model trained intensively on curated, expert-validated data. Think of an SLM as a specialist doctor vs. a generic LLM's generalist.

Aviation SLMs: Built Right

An aviation SLM is trained on:

  • Actual QRH and FCOM manuals from aircraft manufacturers
  • Regulatory documents (EASA technical standards)
  • Case studies from accident investigations (with sensitive data redacted)
  • Expert-validated pilot feedback

Comparative Performance

In our validation study with 300 aviation professionals, we compared responses from:

  • Traditional QRH (reference baseline)
  • GPT-4 with aviation context provided via prompt
  • Our aviation SLM (MADSS)

Scenario: A321 with an engine fire warning during cruise climb.

  • GPT-4 Response: Generated 8 steps. 5 were correct, 2 were questionable (suggested actions not in actual Airbus procedures), 1 was partially incorrect (conflated different engine systems).
  • Aviation SLM Response: Retrieved all 11 correct steps from the actual Airbus A321 QRH, identified related FCOM warnings, flagged a critical MEL item pilots often miss.

Why SLMs Matter for Aviation

Accountability. When an SLM generates guidance, it can cite the specific document section it's drawing from. "Step 3 comes from Airbus A321 QRH, Section 3.2.1." Auditable. Defensible. Compliant.

Regulatory Approval. Aviation regulators (FAA, EASA) are beginning to evaluate AI systems for operational use. A generic LLM will never be approved for safety-critical guidance. An SLM with documented training data, validation results, and audit trails? That's approvable.

Precision. An SLM trained on aviation data understands that "firewall shutoff" in aviation means something very specific. A generic LLM might interpret it as a software firewall. This distinction is critical—and only domain-specific training makes it automatic.

The Future: SLM + Agentic Reasoning

The most powerful approach combines domain-specific SLMs with agentic reasoning. This is MADSS. Instead of just retrieving procedures, an agentic system reasons about procedures, identifies interactions, and flags risks. It's the difference between having access to the reference library vs. having an experienced check captain in the cockpit.

In aviation, as in medicine, law, and other regulated domains, generic beats specific on breadth but loses spectacularly on depth and safety. Domain-specific SLMs aren't the future of AI—they're the baseline for responsible AI in critical domains.

PLS-SEM Validation: Testing MADSS with 300 Pilots

📅 January 2026 ⏱ 7 min read ✍️ Dr. Vikram Narendran

Building an AI system is one thing. Proving it actually works in the real world is another. For MADSS, we needed statistical rigor. Enter PLS-SEM (Partial Least Squares Structural Equation Modeling).

Why PLS-SEM?

Traditional statistical tests assume normal distributions and linear relationships. Real-world aviation data doesn't work that way. PLS-SEM is robust to messy data and handles complex, latent constructs—things you can't measure directly but can measure through multiple indicators.

In our study, we measured constructs like "decision confidence," "procedure completeness," and "cognitive load"—all latent variables with multiple measurement dimensions.

The Study Design

Participants

  • 300 aviation professionals
  • Diverse roles: Captain (40%), First Officer (35%), Flight Engineer (15%), Training Captain (10%)
  • Experience levels: 3,000 to 25,000+ flight hours
  • Aircraft types: A320 family, B737 family, B777, regional jets

Methodology

Each participant was presented with 5 emergency scenarios. For each scenario, they received:

  • Traditional QRH guidance
  • MADSS guidance
  • A questionnaire measuring decision confidence, clarity, time to decision, perceived completeness

Key Findings

Decision Accuracy: MADSS guidance led to 23% fewer procedure errors compared to traditional QRH alone.

Procedure Completeness: Participants using MADSS identified 34% more critical procedural steps than those using QRH alone.

Cognitive Load: Self-reported cognitive load was 18% lower when using MADSS, attributed to the system's highlighting of related procedures and common traps.

Decision Confidence: Pilots reported significantly higher confidence in their decisions when using MADSS—despite the higher volume of information provided. This suggests that agentic guidance (organizing information intelligently) reduces rather than increases cognitive burden.

The Statistical Model

Our PLS-SEM model tested relationships between:

  • AI System Quality (accuracy, completeness, clarity) → Decision Quality
  • Cognitive Support (reduced cognitive load, faster decision time) → Pilot Confidence
  • Decision Quality + Confidence → Perceived Safety Benefit

All pathways were statistically significant (p < 0.05). The model explained 67% of variance in perceived safety benefit—a strong effect.

Why This Matters

Peer-reviewed validation is the currency of academic research and regulatory approval. A claims-backed study convinces no one. A rigorous PLS-SEM analysis with 300 participants across multiple airlines and aircraft types? That's defensible, publishable, and regulatorily credible.

The paper has been accepted at the ATRS World Conference 2026. Full methodology, models, and detailed results will be presented in Beijing.

What's Next for MADSS Validation

We're planning operational trials with actual airline crews in the next phase—not simulations, but real-world deployment in training scenarios. The ultimate validation will be: Does MADSS reduce check-ride failures? Do pilots trained with MADSS handle actual emergencies more safely? Those answers will take time, but that's the research frontier we're entering.

From CAD 1.6M Savings: SVM in Aviation Inventory

📅 December 2025 ⏱ 9 min read ✍️ Dr. Vikram Narendran

Full Flight Simulators require thousands of parts. At any given moment, CAE maintains an inventory of 3,000+ SKUs (stock keeping units) across 20+ simulators. Some parts are critical spares (failure could stop training). Others are consumables. And many are never used.

The challenge: how do you maintain 99.8% part availability while reducing inventory value by 30%?

The answer isn't intuitive. You can't just cut everything proportionally.

The Problem

When I arrived at CAE Dubai (2005-2023), inventory was classified simply: "critical" or "non-critical." This binary approach led to overstocking across the board.

We needed a more nuanced classification. Which parts are truly critical? Which could be sourced faster? Which are obsolete? Which are consumed slowly but absolutely necessary?

Introducing Support Vector Machines (SVM)

SVM is a classification algorithm that excels at finding optimal decision boundaries in high-dimensional space. We trained an SVM on 10 years of historical data to classify parts into categories based on:

  • Usage frequency (annual requisitions)
  • Lead time (time to replenish from supplier)
  • Cost (unit price)
  • Criticality (impact on simulator availability if unavailable)
  • Shelf life / obsolescence risk
  • Supplier reliability

The Classification System

The SVM generated a nuanced classification:

  • Tier 1 (20% of inventory value, 5% of SKUs): Critical spares with high cost and long lead times. Stock aggressively. These are often proprietary parts only available from manufacturers.
  • Tier 2 (30% of inventory value, 15% of SKUs): Important consumables with moderate cost and standard lead times. Stock 60% of historical max.
  • Tier 3 (35% of inventory value, 40% of SKUs): Non-critical maintenance items. Stock on-demand with standing supplier agreements for 48-hour delivery.
  • Tier 4 (15% of inventory value, 40% of SKUs): Slow-moving or obsolete parts. Phase out or reduce to minimal stock.

Results: CAD 1.6M Reduction

  • Inventory value reduced by 43% (CAD 1.6M savings)
  • Part availability maintained at 99.8% despite lower inventory
  • Obsolescence reduced by 55% through better lifecycle management
  • Cash freed up for other operations

How It Worked in Practice

The SVM recommended specific stock levels for each part. Instead of replacing all Tier 4 parts immediately, we implemented a gradual phase-out, monitoring supplier performance. For Tier 1 items, we negotiated better deals with manufacturers, knowing we'd commit to holding more strategic inventory.

Maintenance teams initially resisted—"What if we need this part and it's not in stock?" We addressed this through data. We showed that for 95% of Tier 3 parts, the mean time to supplier delivery was 2 days, so zero-inventory with fast-replenishment was actually safer than holding dead stock.

Key Learnings

1. Context Matters

The SVM worked because we engineered features that reflected real-world complexity. "Just use part cost and lead time" would have been naive. We included supplier reliability, criticality, and obsolescence risk—business knowledge that data scientists alone wouldn't capture.

2. Organizational Adoption Requires Buy-In

The technical work (SVM training) took 2 weeks. Getting operations teams to trust and implement the recommendations took 3 months. We did workshops, showed historical examples, addressed concerns. Change management was the bottleneck, not the algorithm.

3. Optimization Isn't Binary

We didn't optimize for minimum cost or maximum availability separately. We optimized for the business trade-off: maintain 99.8% availability at minimum cost. SVM's ability to weight multiple objectives made this possible.

The full case study was published in the American Journal of Industrial and Business Management (May 2024). DOI: 10.4236/ajibm.2024.145040

25 Years in FSTD: Lessons from the Simulator Floor

📅 November 2025 ⏱ 11 min read ✍️ Dr. Vikram Narendran

I've spent a quarter-century in Full Flight Simulator operations. Jet Airways in Mumbai (2000-2005). Emirates-CAE in Dubai (2005-2023). CAE in Dallas (2023-present). Across three continents, multiple airlines, and diverse teams. If there's one thing I've learned, it's this: technical excellence matters, but culture matters more.

Lesson 1: Regulatory Compliance Is Alignment, Not Burden

When I started, compliance was seen as friction—inspectors were adversaries, regulations were obstacles. I watched teams hide problems instead of fixing them. Audit failures followed inevitably.

I learned to flip the narrative. Regulators (EASA, FAA, etc.) aren't enemies. They're stakeholders in the same mission: safe aviation. When a new regulation arrived, I'd gather the team and say: "Here's what the regulator is trying to prevent. How do we not just comply, but *exceed* this standard?"

Result: In my 18 years at Emirates-CAE, we achieved 100% audit pass rate across all global authorities. Not by gaming the system, but by building compliance into every process. Teams took pride in this.

Lesson 2: Technical Depth Builds Team Respect

My teams have always mixed experience levels. Fresh technicians working alongside senior engineers with 30+ years of experience. The temptation is for leadership to delegate to the most experienced and check out.

Instead, I chose to stay technically sharp. I learned Linux. I debugged C code. I ran QTG tests. When a complex motion system issue arose, I didn't call a vendor—I worked it myself, alongside the team.

Why? Because technical credibility is the foundation of leadership in engineering. Your team will never fully trust directives from someone they suspect doesn't understand the work. But if they've seen you sweat through a problem, they'll follow your guidance anywhere.

Lesson 3: Solving Old Problems Wins Hearts

Every simulator facility has legacy issues. At Emirates-CAE, a Hawker 800 had a software discrepancy that had existed for 10 years. Multiple vendors had looked at it. All said it was unfixable without rewriting massive C code. The workaround had become accepted.

I made it a personal mission. For 3 months, I dug into the code, traced the logic, understood why the discrepancy occurred. Found a surgical fix that required 47 lines of code rewrite. Within weeks, the discrepancy was gone.

Did this save millions? No. But it earned something more valuable: the trust and loyalty of every engineer on my team. They saw that I didn't accept "that's how it's always been." They learned to think the same way.

Lesson 4: Knowledge Transfer is Non-Negotiable

When I arrived at CAE Dallas, I realized the organization had hidden knowledge—wisdom locked in individual engineers' heads. If they left, the knowledge left.

I established a mandatory knowledge transfer protocol. Senior engineers documented procedures. Juniors studied them. We held weekly "deep dives" where complex issues were discussed openly, with documentation. I even wrote training modules in Linux and C for the team.

This investment paid dividends. When people retired or left for other opportunities, there was no loss of institutional knowledge. New team members ramp faster. The organization becomes more resilient.

Lesson 5: Culture Beats Process

I've seen highly detailed processes fail spectacularly, and informal cultures succeed brilliantly. The difference? Shared values.

At Emirates-CAE, I established three core values:

  • Safety First: No schedule override, no cost savings, no customer pressure overrides safety. Ever.
  • Transparency: Problems surface immediately, not hidden until they explode. Mistakes are learning opportunities, not career threats.
  • Excellence: We don't do "good enough." Every task—whether fixing a motion system or updating documentation—is done with pride.

These weren't written on a poster. They were lived. I modeled them. Team leads reinforced them. When someone cut corners, we didn't punish—we talked about how it conflicted with our values. Over time, the culture self-reinforced.

Lesson 6: AI and Automation Enhance Humans, Not Replace Them

As I've moved into AI/ML work, I'm acutely aware of the fear automation creates. Teams worry: "Will predictive maintenance eliminate maintenance jobs?"

The answer is nuanced. Predictive maintenance changes jobs, not eliminates them. Instead of reactive troubleshooting (which anyone can do), maintenance teams shift to strategic planning, optimization, and exception handling (which require skill and judgment).

I always introduce AI tools with this framing. "This system handles routine predictions so you can focus on complex problems and strategic improvements." Some teams embrace this. Others resist. The ones that embrace it become more valuable to the organization. The ones that resist become obsolete.

My job as a leader is to help people see themselves in the future, not fear it.

Lesson 7: Listen More, Decide Less

Early in my career, I confused leadership with decisiveness. I'd gather facts quickly and move to decisions. I thought listening too long was indecision.

Over 25 years, I learned that the best decisions come from understanding diverse perspectives. When a junior technician raises a concern, they've usually seen something I missed. When a seasoned engineer suggests a workaround, they've learned it from patterns I haven't observed.

Now, I listen first. Decide second. And I'm rarely wrong. The 10 minutes of listening prevents 10 hours of correcting mistakes.

Lesson 8: Purpose Outlasts Profit

Flying is extraordinary. A 500-ton aluminum tube hurtles through the sky at 900 km/h, carrying hundreds of lives. This only works because of training. And training only works because simulators work flawlessly.

I've never done this work for a paycheck (though I'm grateful for one). I've done it because the stakes are real. Every simulator I maintain contributes to safety. Every procedure I optimize might save lives.

When I'm training junior engineers, I always remind them: "You're not fixing a machine. You're keeping pilots sharp and passengers safe." Purpose transcends profit. People who understand this are the ones who excel.

The Future

25 years in, I'm more excited about aviation than ever. AI, autonomous systems, new aircraft architectures—the industry is in flux. My job is to guide teams through this transition, helping them see themselves as valuable in the new world, and building safety-first cultures that persist through change.

That's the real work of leadership.

If you're early in an engineering career, remember: technical skills get you in the door, but culture, leadership, and purpose are what sustain decades-long excellence.
